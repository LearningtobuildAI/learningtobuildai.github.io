Human Circulatory System

What it is

A complex network of organs and systems that work together to transport nutrients, oxygen, hormones, and waste products throughout the body.

What it does

The circulatory system plays a crucial role in maintaining homeostasis by regulating temperature, pH, and providing essential components for cellular metabolism. It also helps protect the body from pathogens and toxins.

How it works

1. Blood flow through the heart: The heart, serving as the central pump of the circulatory system, pumps blood into two main networks: the pulmonary circuit (to the lungs) and the systemic circuit (to the rest of the body).

2. Blood vessels: Blood vessels carry blood throughout the body. Arteries transport blood away from the heart, while veins return it back to the heart. Capillaries, the smallest blood vessels, connect arteries and veins and facilitate exchange between tissues and blood.

3. Cardiac cycle: The heart's four chambers—the left and right atria (upper chambers) and left and right ventricles (lower chambers)—work in a synchronized fashion to maintain blood flow.

4. Blood pressure: Blood pressure is created by the force of blood against the walls of arteries, resulting from the heart's pumping action and resistance offered by the peripheral circulatory system.

Materials required

1. Heart: The muscular organ responsible for pumping blood through the circulatory system.

2. Blood vessels: Includes arteries, veins, and capillaries that carry blood throughout the body.

3. Blood: A liquid connective tissue comprised of plasma (a watery fluid), red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. It carries nutrients, oxygen, hormones, and waste products between tissues and organs.

4. Lymphatic system: A network of vessels and organs that collects excess fluids and proteins from the interstitial spaces (between cells) and returns them to the bloodstream.

Process to make

The circulatory system forms during embryonic development. The heart begins as a single tube, which eventually differentiates into four chambers by the end of week 9. Blood vessels develop from specialized mesodermal cells called angioplasty, which migrate and aggregate to form blood vessel walls.

Energy needed

The circulatory system requires a substantial amount of energy to maintain its function. The pumping action of the heart consumes approximately 70% of total body oxygen during rest, with the remaining energy used for transporting nutrients, maintaining temperature, and defending against pathogens.

Additional sections (optional)

1. The lymphatic system: A crucial component of the circulatory system, responsible for maintaining fluid balance and immune function.

2. Blood clotting: The process by which blood transforms from a liquid to a solid in response to injury, preventing excessive bleeding.

3. Regulation of body temperature: The circulatory system plays a significant role in maintaining the body's core temperature through the distribution of heat and cold-regulating hormones.

4. Blood pressure regulation: Various mechanisms contribute to maintaining stable blood pressure, including the autonomic nervous system and hormonal regulators such as renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS).
