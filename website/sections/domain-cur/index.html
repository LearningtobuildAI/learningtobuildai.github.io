Quantum Computing — Technology Landscape
Superconducting QubitsNEAR

IBM Condor (1,000+ qubits), Google Willow (105 qubits — first below-threshold error correction), Rigetti, Oxford Quantum Circuits. The dominant commercial approach — requires millikelvin cooling. Google Willow solved a benchmark calculation in 5 minutes that would take 10 septillion years on today's supercomputers.

IBM QUANTUM · GOOGLE QUANTUM AI · RIGETTI · OXFORD QUANTUM CIRCUITS
Trapped Ion QubitsNEAR

IonQ (Aria, Forte) and Quantinuum (H2) achieving the highest gate fidelities of any quantum platform — 99.9%+ two-qubit fidelity. Slower gate speeds than superconducting but far fewer errors. Universal Quantum working on magnetic trap scaling. Operates near room temperature with ion traps.

IONQ (College Park, MD) · QUANTINUUM (Broomfield, CO) · UNIVERSAL QUANTUM (Brighton, UK)
Photonic Quantum ComputingNEAR

PsiQuantum (Silicon Valley) targeting a million-qubit photonic chip using standard semiconductor fab processes. Xanadu's Borealis demonstrated quantum advantage in Gaussian boson sampling 2022. Operates at room temperature — major advantage. Key challenge: photon loss in waveguides.

PSIQUANTUM (Palo Alto, CA) · XANADU (Toronto, CA) · QUIX QUANTUM (Enschede, NL)
Neutral Atom ArraysNEAR

QuEra's Aquila (256 qubits), Pasqal, and Atom Computing demonstrating the fastest-scaling qubit count growth in 2024–25. Individual atoms held in optical tweezers — reconfigurable mid-circuit. Harvard/MIT 2023: 48 logical qubits from 280 physical qubits using transversal gates — a landmark in error correction.

QUERA (Boston, MA) · PASQAL (Paris, France) · ATOM COMPUTING (Berkeley, CA)
Topological QubitsFAR

Microsoft Azure Quantum's bet on Majorana zero modes — exotic quasiparticles theoretically providing built-in error protection. If achieved, topological qubits could be inherently error-free, requiring far fewer physical qubits per logical qubit than any other approach. Microsoft announced first Majorana 1 chip in 2025.

MICROSOFT AZURE QUANTUM (Redmond, WA) · DELFT UNIVERSITY (Netherlands)
Quantum Error CorrectionFAR

The key threshold to fault-tolerant quantum computing. Surface codes, color codes, and LDPC codes allow logical qubits to be protected from decoherence. Google Willow crossed below-threshold for the first time in 2024. Fault-tolerant machines need 1,000–10,000 physical qubits per logical qubit — scaling the central challenge of the 2020s.
