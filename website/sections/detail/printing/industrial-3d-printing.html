1Definition

Industrial 3D printing, an advanced form of additive manufacturing, involves the creation of three-dimensional objects by depositing materials in layers based on digital model data. This technology allows for the production of complex geometries and customized parts with high precision.

2How It Works

The process begins with a CAD (Computer-Aided Design) file that defines the object's geometry. The printer then uses this digital blueprint to lay down successive layers of material, such as polymers, metals, or ceramics, fusing them together until the complete part is formed.

3Problem It Solves

Traditional manufacturing methods often require extensive tooling and setup for producing complex geometries, which can be time-consuming and costly. Industrial 3D printing eliminates these constraints by enabling the direct production of parts from digital designs without the need for additional tools or molds.

4Manufacturing / Creation Process

Industrial 3D printers are typically large-scale machines that operate in industrial settings. They require a controlled environment to maintain consistent temperature, humidity, and air quality during operation.

5Materials Used
  • polymers
  • metals
  • ceramics
6Build Process

The build process involves several steps: preparing the CAD model, selecting the appropriate material and setting parameters such as layer thickness and infill density, loading the printer with materials, initiating the print job, and post-processing the printed part to remove supports and finish it.

16Glossary
CAD (Computer-Aided Design)
A technology that allows the creation and modification of geometric models and technical documentation using computers.
layer thickness
The height of each layer deposited during a 3D printing process, which affects the resolution and build time of the final object.
infill density
The percentage of material filled within the internal structure of a printed part, impacting strength and weight.
