Human Nervous System

What it is

An integral part of the human body, the nervous system serves as the primary communication network, connecting our brain to every organ and cell. It's responsible for receiving and processing sensory information, coordinating responses, and regulating bodily functions.

What it does

1. Sensory Function: Receives stimuli from the environment through various senses (vision, hearing, touch, etc.) and internal organs.

2. Motor Control: Facilitates muscle movements in response to sensory input or brain signals.

3. Regulation of bodily functions: Regulates vital processes such as breathing, heart rate, digestion, and hormone secretion.

4. Learning and Memory: Influences cognitive abilities, allowing the brain to learn from experiences and form memories.

How it works

The nervous system is divided into two main parts: the central nervous system (CNS - brain and spinal cord) and the peripheral nervous system (PNS - nerves outside the CNS). Neurons are the primary cells that make up the nervous system, sending and receiving electrochemical signals via dendrites, axons, and synapses.

Materials required

1. Neurons: Primary cells of the nervous system responsible for sending and receiving electrical impulses.

2. Axons and Dendrites: Extensions of neurons that transmit signals between neurons or from neurons to muscles or glands.

3. Synapses: Junctions where two neurons connect, allowing the transmission of electrical or chemical signals between them.

4. Myelin Sheath: A fatty substance surrounding some axons in the peripheral nervous system, facilitating faster and more efficient signal transmission.

5. Neurotransmitters: Chemical messengers that allow for communication between neurons at synapses.

Process to make

The human nervous system develops from a neural tube during embryonic development, later differentiating into the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves. Neuron production continues throughout life in certain areas of the brain. The structure and connections between neurons are shaped by experiences and learning throughout an individual's lifetime.

Energy needed

The human nervous system is highly metabolically active due to its constant signaling and regulation of bodily functions. It accounts for approximately 20% of the body's total energy expenditure, with glucose being the primary source of energy for neurons.
